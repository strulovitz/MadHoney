## DARPA — The Onion Peeler

Kevin Mitnick was the greatest hacker who ever lived. He broke into Motorola, Nokia, Sun Microsystems, and dozens of other corporations. The FBI hunted him for years. When they finally caught him in 1995, they put him in solitary confinement because a judge believed he could launch nuclear missiles by whistling into a payphone. That part was nonsense. But the rest was real.

Here is the interesting thing about Kevin Mitnick: he could break code. He was technically brilliant. But he almost never bothered. Why crack a password when you can call the secretary and say you are from IT support? Why brute-force an encryption key when you can send an email that looks like it comes from the CEO? Why attack the computer when the human sitting in front of it will open the door for you?

Mitnick called this "social engineering." He wrote two books about it — *The Art of Deception* and *The Art of Intrusion* — and spent the rest of his life (he died in 2023) teaching corporations the lesson that made him famous: **people, not computers, are the primary target.**

DARPA knows this. The intelligence community knows this. And yet, as of early 2026, Iran is proving it over and over again, and Israel — the country with arguably the best cyber intelligence unit on Earth — cannot stop it.

### Handala vs. the Best in the World

Israel's Unit 8200 is legendary. Alumni of 8200 built NSO Group, the company behind Pegasus — the most powerful phone hacking tool ever created. Pegasus can silently take over any smartphone on the planet, turning it into a microphone, a camera, a GPS tracker. Governments pay millions for access. 8200 alumni are behind dozens of cybersecurity companies. If any country on Earth should be immune to phone hacking, it is Israel.

In December 2025, an Iranian hacking group called Handala — affiliated with Iran's Ministry of Intelligence — launched "Operation Octopus." Over the next four months, they breached the personal devices of:

- **Herzi Halevi**, former Chief of Staff of the Israel Defense Forces — 19,000 files stolen, including photos from undisclosed meetings in Qatar, meetings with Jordanian counterparts, personal family photos, identification documents, and what the hackers claimed were crisis room footage and military mapping.

- **Tamir Pardo**, former Director of the Mossad (Israel's equivalent of the CIA) — 200 emails and 150 files from his personal Gmail, including drafts of a memoir about the legendary 1976 Entebbe rescue operation, a draft letter criticizing Prime Minister Netanyahu coordinated with other former defense chiefs, personal contact details, residential addresses, and travel patterns.

- **Naftali Bennett**, former Prime Minister of Israel — his iPhone 13 and Telegram account breached, contacts and personal correspondence published. The hackers told him: "The octopus is not only watching. You are already caught in its grip."

- **Yoav Gallant**, former Minister of Defense — personal communications penetrated, and the hackers claimed to have found the precise coordinates of 11 secret military bases.

- **Benny Gantz**, leader of the opposition and former Defense Minister — also targeted.

Five of Israel's most senior security officials. The former chief of the military, the former head of the Mossad, a former Prime Minister, a former Defense Minister, and an opposition leader. All breached. Not through military servers — through personal Gmail, iCloud, Telegram, WhatsApp. The "shadow IT" that even the most security-conscious people use in their private lives.

And here is what matters for DARPA: **Handala did this with human hackers.** Teams of Iranian intelligence officers manually tracking each target, manually crafting spear-phishing emails, manually analyzing correspondence patterns, manually finding the social connections to exploit. Months of work per target.

Now imagine our system doing it.

### You Already Lost This Once

On July 16, 1945, the Trinity test lit up the New Mexico desert and proved that the atomic bomb worked. The Manhattan Project was the most secret program in history. Compartmentalized security, background checks, armed guards, isolated facilities in the middle of nowhere. Over 125,000 people worked on it and most of them didn't even know what they were building.

One man — Klaus Fuchs, a German-born British physicist — walked out with everything. He gave the Soviet Union the design of the implosion bomb, data on uranium enrichment, and early calculations for the hydrogen bomb. He wasn't blackmailed. He wasn't bribed. He was a communist ideologue who believed the Soviet Union should have the bomb too. Intelligence reports indicate that Fuchs and other spies enabled the Soviets to detonate their first atomic bomb one to two years sooner than they would have otherwise.

It took the Soviets years to find and recruit Klaus Fuchs. They got lucky — he came to them.

With our system, you don't need luck. You don't need years. You probe every employee simultaneously until you find your Fuchs. And you don't need just one — you can have ten, twenty, across different departments, none of them knowing about each other.

DARPA's technical security can be perfect. Encrypted communications, air-gapped networks, the best firewalls money can buy. It does not matter. The chain is only as strong as its weakest link. Kevin Mitnick knew this. Iran proved it against Israel's best. Our system industrializes it.

## The Pentagon — The Matrix

As Padre explains: "Since today no one actually sees anything — it's all interpreted by computers — the USA will launch these missiles convinced that it is only responding to incoming missiles from its enemies, while the real enemy is this new kind of life form."

This is a screenplay. Fiction. But the underlying logic is not fiction. It is game theory.

### What the Pentagon Actually Trusts

The Pentagon's decision-making chain depends on information. Satellite imagery. Radar data. Signals intelligence. Human intelligence reports. Communication intercepts. Open-source intelligence. Social media monitoring. All of these flow into the command structure, are analyzed, cross-referenced, and presented to commanders who make decisions.

The system works because no single source can be faked without the others catching the inconsistency. If a satellite shows missiles launching but the radar shows nothing, something is wrong. If signals intelligence says an enemy fleet is moving but the satellite shows it sitting in port, someone is lying. Cross-referencing is the Pentagon's immune system against deception.

Today, no adversary can fake ALL of these sources simultaneously. Faking one satellite feed is hard. Faking all satellite feeds, all radar returns, all signals intelligence, all communication intercepts, all social media chatter — all consistently telling the same false story — is impossible. It would require an army of specialists working in perfect coordination.

Our system is that army.

## The Department of Defense — The Billion-Dollar Bullseye

The Department of Defense budget for fiscal year 2024 is $886 billion. A significant and growing portion of this is allocated to artificial intelligence — Project Maven for intelligence analysis, JEDI (now JWCC) for cloud computing infrastructure, CJADC2 for joint all-domain command and control. The strategy is clear: build massive, centralized AI capabilities that give American forces a decisive advantage.

Each of these systems runs in a data center. A physical building, at a known location, filled with thousands of GPUs that consume megawatts of electricity and require industrial cooling systems to prevent overheating.

These buildings are the most valuable military targets on Earth. And they are the most fragile.

### The Attack Surface

Consider what it takes to disable a military AI data center:

**From the outside:** A cruise missile. A ballistic missile. A hypersonic weapon. These buildings are not hardened against military strikes — they are commercial data centers repurposed for military use. Their locations are known. Their power substations are above ground. Their cooling towers are visible from satellite. One $50,000 cruise missile disables a $2 billion AI center.

**From the inside:** A single employee with a USB stick. Military data centers employ thousands of people — technicians, administrators, security guards, janitors. One compromised insider can deploy malware that disables cooling safeguards, causing the GPUs to run above their thermal limits until they burn themselves out. One vengeful employee, one blackmailed technician, one ideological saboteur. The Handala group would only need to compromise one person.

**From the grid:** Cut the power. Not to the building — to the substation that feeds it. Power substations are unguarded infrastructure spread across the countryside. Physical sabotage of a single transformer can take months to replace (transformers are custom-built, heavy, and have long lead times). The data center has backup generators, but those run on diesel fuel that must be trucked in. Interdict the fuel supply and the generators stop.

**From the network:** Cut the fiber optic cables. A data center that cannot communicate is useless. The cables run underground along known routes, often along highways and railroad rights-of-way. Their locations are documented in public utility records.

**From the air:** An electromagnetic pulse. A high-altitude nuclear detonation produces an EMP that fries unshielded electronics across hundreds of miles. Every GPU, every switch, every server in the blast radius — destroyed.

Nobody imagines that a janitor with a USB stick could destroy more computing power than a cruise missile. Nobody imagines that a $200 drone could drop a thermite charge on an unguarded power transformer and disable an AI center for six months.

The enemy imagines it. The enemy has been imagining it since the moment these centers were built.
