## I. Europe's Regulators Actually Work

The first thing American executives get wrong about the European Commission is that they imagine it is the same kind of institution as the United States Food and Drug Administration, or the Federal Trade Commission, or the Federal Communications Commission — a captured regulator, politely going through the motions, ultimately serving the industries it is supposed to oversee. American executives imagine this because it is what they are used to at home, and human beings always assume the institutions they are negotiating with are shaped like the institutions they know.

This assumption is wrong.

The European Commission is not captured. The revolving door between industry and regulator in Brussels is smaller, slower, and more politically expensive than the one in Washington. European regulators are career civil servants, not political appointees rotating back to consulting firms. The European Commission has a documented track record of regulating powerful American industries **on behalf of European citizens,** not on behalf of the industries. You do not have to trust me on this — you have to read the record.

**Genetically modified food.** Europe kept genetically modified food out of the European food supply for the better part of two decades, over the active lobbying of Monsanto, the US Trade Representative, and several American administrations. The precautionary principle held. European children do not eat Bt-toxin corn every day the way American children do. **Someone in Brussels cared enough to say no to the most powerful agribusiness lobby on Earth, and the no held.** The Pusztai affair I described in Chapter 4 of this book happened in *Aberdeen*, not in Brussels. Aberdeen fired the scientist in 48 hours after a phone call from Monsanto to Clinton to Blair. Brussels would have protected him. That difference is the difference between a captured regulator and a real one.

**GDPR.** The General Data Protection Regulation, passed in 2016 and enforced from 2018 forward, is the most aggressive data privacy law in the Western world. It has produced real fines — billions of euros against Google, Meta, Amazon, and a long list of smaller American tech companies — for actual privacy violations. Every American tech company has a GDPR compliance team, not because GDPR is good public relations but because GDPR fines are a real line on the quarterly earnings report. GDPR was not designed to protect American tech revenue. GDPR was designed to protect European citizens from American tech, and it has worked in proportion to how enforceable it is.

**Digital Markets Act and Digital Services Act.** Passed in 2022. Designated several American tech giants as "gatekeepers" — a legal status with specific compliance obligations. Again: real law, real enforcement, real fines, real structural consequences for the regulated parties.

**The EU AI Act.** Passed in March 2024. The world's first comprehensive legal framework for artificial intelligence. It establishes risk tiers (unacceptable risk, high risk, limited risk, minimal risk). It bans unacceptable uses outright — social scoring, biometric mass surveillance in public spaces, manipulation of vulnerable populations. It imposes transparency and accountability requirements on high-risk AI systems. It is a real law, passed by a real legislature, signed by a real commission, enforced by real regulators who report to real member states. **The law exists. It is on the books.**

The track record is consistent: when Brussels has the legal authority and the technical means to regulate an American industry on behalf of European citizens, Brussels does it. Unlike Washington. Unlike the FDA. Unlike the regulatory apparatus that you, the American reader, are used to bribing, lobbying, and revolving-door-ing into submission. **Brussels is the one major Western regulator that you cannot buy, and this fact is the entire reason this chapter matters.**

## II. The AI Act Is a Dead Letter — Until Now

Here is the part of the story that Brussels would rather you did not understand clearly.

The EU AI Act is a beautiful law. It is well-drafted, technically informed, politically courageous, and in principle enforceable. It is also, as of the date this chapter is being written, almost completely ineffective in practice. The risk-tier framework is theoretical. The transparency requirements are theoretical. The gatekeeper-adjacent provisions are theoretical. The audits are theoretical. The fines, so far, are theoretical.

Why?

**Because Brussels wrote a law against a monopoly that Europe has no power to replace.**

You cannot effectively regulate an industry when every major player in that industry is headquartered in a foreign country and when the regulated European population has no alternative except to use the regulated product. The AI Act is a law against American cloud AI — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Microsoft, Meta, Amazon — and every European citizen, every European hospital, every European law firm, every European university, every European government department, uses American cloud AI because there is no other option. The only option for a European who wants to use AI is to route through one of the five or six American companies the AI Act was written to constrain.

A law against a monopoly is not enforceable when the regulated population depends on the monopoly to function. **This is the specific condition the American cloud AI industry has been relying on for the past two years to make the AI Act toothless.** The Act exists. The fines are written. The audits are scheduled. But every time a European regulator draws the sword, the regulator realizes that cutting off OpenAI means cutting off the European hospital that uses OpenAI for radiology, the European law firm that uses OpenAI for contract review, the European translator that uses OpenAI for real-time translation, the European scientist that uses OpenAI for literature review. The sword stays in its sheath because using the sword costs more than the regulator can afford.

**This is the condition that is about to change.**

The AI Act was written assuming that, sooner or later, Europe would develop or obtain a real alternative to American cloud AI — a substitute Europe could route its own usage through, so that Brussels could actually enforce its own law. The assumption was reasonable. The assumption was wrong for two years. ## III. Mistral, Aleph Alpha, and the Failed European Sovereign AI Project

Europe has been trying to build a European alternative to American cloud AI since 2021. The best European candidate is **Mistral AI** — a French company founded in 2023 by former DeepMind and Meta researchers. Mistral publishes open-weight models (Mixtral, Codestral, and several more recent releases) that are competent but not frontier. Mistral is structurally several generations behind OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic in terms of scale, training compute, and model capability. This is not because the Mistral team is not talented. It is because frontier scale requires ten to twenty billion dollars of training compute and the French equity markets are not structured to write that check.

The second European candidate is **Aleph Alpha** — a German AI company headquartered in Heidelberg, producing a family of models called Luminous. Aleph Alpha is smaller than Mistral by every metric. Most people who follow the AI industry closely have never heard of it. Most American executives reading this chapter will not have heard of it before this sentence. Aleph Alpha is real, Aleph Alpha is serious, Aleph Alpha is staffed by competent people, and Aleph Alpha is not going to compete with OpenAI at frontier scale.

Every other European "sovereign AI" initiative you have heard of — French government GPU investments, German national computing clusters, the EU supercomputer programs, the various sovereign wealth funds investing in European AI — is **either dependent on American hardware (primarily NVIDIA), or running American research methods with a European flag taped on top, or both.** The sovereign AI project in Europe has been trying to build an independent European alternative for the better part of five years, and the honest assessment as of 2026 is: the project is not catching up.

**This is not Europe's fault.** Frontier-scale AI requires capital at a rate that European equity markets are not structured to deliver, and regulatory environments that European competition law is not structured to produce, and compute access that European hardware exports cannot economically secure at scale. The United States does AI at frontier scale because the United States has concentrated enormous amounts of capital in a small number of private companies over a period of years, and because American venture capital is willing to light hundreds of billions of dollars on fire on long bets. Europe does not have that capital culture. Europe was never going to have that capital culture. Europe needs a different path to sovereign AI than the one Silicon Valley has built, because the path Silicon Valley has built requires conditions Europe does not produce.

Brussels knows this. Brussels has been quietly watching the Mistral funding rounds, the Aleph Alpha struggles, the EuroHPC supercomputer initiatives, the Gefion investments in Denmark, and Brussels has been waiting for a different path to appear. **The different path has now appeared.**

## V. The ASML Analogy

The dependency pattern in what I just described is not new. Europe has lived with analogous dependencies in other critical industries for decades, and Europe knows how to manage them without losing sovereignty. Consider how Europe's relationship to semiconductor manufacturing works.

**ASML** is a Dutch company — headquartered in Veldhoven, in the southern Netherlands — that makes extreme ultraviolet lithography machines. These are the machines used to manufacture the most advanced semiconductors on Earth. Without ASML's EUV machines, Taiwan's TSMC cannot produce the chips in your iPhone, Samsung cannot produce the chips in your Korean refrigerator, Intel cannot produce its most advanced processors, and China cannot manufacture cutting-edge AI accelerators no matter how much money Beijing throws at the problem. **ASML is a monopoly.** ASML's EUV lithography is the single most important chokepoint in the global semiconductor supply chain, and the ongoing story of Chinese industrial espionage around ASML is one of the most important sub-plots in the US-China trade war.

Europe lives with ASML as a dependency. Every European government that cares about semiconductors knows that the Netherlands effectively controls a critical piece of the supply chain, and every European member state negotiates with Amsterdam accordingly. The dependency is real, the dependency is structural, and the dependency has not caused Europe to lose sovereignty over its own chip-using industries, because the ASML machines are physically located in Taiwanese and Korean and European fabs, not in Dutch ones. **The Dutch supplier is a supplier. The operations are local. The sovereignty is operational even where the dependency is real.**

**Europe's relationship to Chinese open-weight foundation models will be structurally identical to Europe's relationship to ASML — except inverted.**

DeepSeek and Qwen are the Chinese "ASML" of AI foundation models. They are the thing Europe will depend on because Europe cannot build equivalents itself in the relevant time frame. The dependency is real. The dependency will be permanent for the foreseeable future. **But the dependency is operationally sovereign, in exactly the same way Europe's dependency on ASML-manufactured semiconductors is operationally sovereign.** The model weights are downloaded to European hardware. Europe runs the inference. Europe audits the outputs. Europe enforces its own rules on how the weights are used. Europe cannot control what DeepSeek or Qwen train next, but Europe can control everything that happens downstream of a model download — and downstream is where all the regulatorily-interesting activity happens.

This is the same structural arrangement a European cancer hospital has with an American-manufactured MRI scanner. The scanner was built by GE or Siemens in a foreign facility. The hospital does not control GE's R&D. But the hospital operates the scanner, the hospital owns the scans, the hospital decides who sees the images, and the hospital is sovereign in its patient care regardless of where the hardware was manufactured. **A foreign dependency, honestly declared, operationally sovereign.** This is how Europe already handles a dozen critical foreign dependencies, and this is how Europe will handle DeepSeek and Qwen.

**Brussels will not be embarrassed by the dependency.** Brussels will be relieved that the dependency replaces a worse dependency — the dependency on American cloud AI that is actively hostile to European regulatory goals — with a better one, on Chinese model weights that Europe can actually audit inside its own jurisdiction. The political narrative writes itself. The European press will celebrate "European AI sovereignty" the week after the first major deployment, regardless of the fact that the underlying model weights are Chinese. Sovereignty is a deployment question, not a training question.
