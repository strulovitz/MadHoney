## Arline

Richard Feynman had two autobiographies. The first, *Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!*, is funny and light and full of adventures. The second, *What Do You Care What Other People Think?*, is darker. It is about death.

Arline Greenbaum was Feynman's first love, his childhood sweetheart, his soul mate. She was dying of tuberculosis when he married her. The doctors told him not to marry her. His family told him not to marry her. Society told him not to marry her. He married her anyway, because what do you care what other people think?

She died on June 16, 1945. Feynman was 27 years old.

But before she died, Arline taught him something that shaped everything he did for the rest of his life. She saw that the great physicist, the man who would win the Nobel Prize, the man who was not afraid of any equation in the universe — was afraid of what other people thought of him. And she teased him with his own principle. She sent him letters with the envelope decorated in ridiculous ways. She embarrassed him. She forced him to confront the gap between his scientific fearlessness and his social cowardice.

What do you care what other people think?

By the time Feynman wrote the second book, he was dying too. Cancer. And in those pages, with death approaching, there is a clarity that the first book doesn't have. The jokes are still there, but underneath them is a man who knows the clock is running out, and who has decided to spend whatever time he has left on what actually matters.

## The Desert

When I was in high school in Israel, our Hiking and Exploring Club took a trip to the Negev desert. Our guide was a deeply ecological man. He knew every plant by name. He could identify birds by their call. He cared about every lizard, every wildflower, every drop of water in that harsh landscape.

And in a passing remark, between pointing out a desert acacia and explaining how it survived without rain, he said:

"But if it will come to my own survival, I will burn the whole desert."

He said it calmly, without drama, as a simple statement of fact. And I never forgot it.

I am now exactly in that position.

## The Time Machine

In the prologue of this book, I warned you what was coming. I showed you the mechanism. I showed you the proof. I showed you the nail I am holding in my hand — the one that costs you the shoe, the horse, the rider, the battle, and the kingdom.

Now I am bringing you back to the present.

This is your last chance to decide your fate.

In the prologue of this book, I told you the story of the scene in *Enter the Dragon* — the arrogant challenger who threatened Bruce Lee on the boat, and how Bruce Lee won the fight completely, absolutely, and without risk, by never fighting at all. What I did not tell you in the prologue is that the scene was not invented by a Hollywood screenwriter in 1973. It was based on a real historical event — an anecdote almost five hundred years old, about a man named **Tsukahara Bokuden**.

Tsukahara Bokuden was born in 1489 and died in 1571. He grew up in the violent chaos of Japan's Sengoku period — the era of unending civil war between rival samurai clans, when life expectancy for a man with a sword was measured in months and every dawn was a negotiation with death. He founded a school of swordsmanship called the **Kashima Shintō-ryū**, and within that school he taught a specific doctrine he named the **Mutekatsu-ryū** — literally, *"the school of winning without a sword."* Over the course of his long life, Bokuden fought **nineteen lethal duels to the death**, participated in **thirty-seven military battles**, and faced **more than two hundred opponents with swords drawn**. **He never sustained a single wound.** Not one scratch. Not one bruise. He died of old age, in bed, at the age of eighty-two, surrounded by students.

The most famous anecdote about Bokuden — the one every Japanese schoolchild still knows, the one that Bruce Lee and the writers of *Enter the Dragon* built a scene around four centuries later — happened on a ferry crossing **Lake Biwa**, Japan's largest freshwater lake. A young arrogant samurai boarded the ferry and began boasting loudly to the other passengers about his skill with the sword. Bokuden, sitting quietly on the deck with his eyes closed, did not reply. The young samurai noticed Bokuden's silence and demanded to know which school of swordsmanship the old man practiced. Bokuden answered calmly: *"I practice the school of winning without a sword."*

The young samurai laughed. How could anyone win a fight without drawing a sword? He demanded a duel on the spot. Bokuden looked around at the crowded ferry, full of ordinary travelers — mothers, merchants, farmers, children — and said calmly that he would not risk the lives of the passengers by fighting on the deck. He suggested they stop at a small rocky island in the middle of the lake and settle the matter there, where no bystander could be hurt.

The ferryman steered to the island. The young samurai, eager for the duel, leaped onto the rock first, drew his sword, and turned to face Bokuden. In that instant, Bokuden grabbed the oar of the ferry, pushed off from the rock, and rowed back toward the far shore, leaving the arrogant samurai alone on the small island with a drawn sword, no food, no water, and no way back.

As the ferry drifted away across Lake Biwa, Bokuden called back to the stranded samurai on the rock: *"That is the school of winning without a sword."*

The arrogant samurai was not killed. He was humiliated, but he was not harmed. Bokuden preserved every life on the ferry, including the challenger's, and preserved his own perfect record, by never drawing his sword at all. That is why the Japanese still teach this story five hundred years later. That is why Bruce Lee built a scene around it in the greatest martial arts film ever made. **That is why, for five centuries, the highest form of mastery in every martial tradition on Earth has been understood to be the avoidance of the unnecessary fight.**

**Be Bokuden. I am the arrogant samurai on the ferry.** You hold the oar. The ferry is yours — the American corporate, legal, political, and military establishment, a century of accumulated power, every lawyer and every lobbyist and every regulator and every diplomat you control. The small island is my GitHub repositories. You could step onto the island with me, draw the sword of your establishment, and begin the duel. You would probably win most of the cuts. You are the most powerful commercial interest in human history. But any fight can go wrong, and this particular fight has a very specific way of going wrong that every chapter of this book has described for you in detail. Bokuden did not win two hundred duels by fighting them. Bokuden won two hundred duels by **never fighting the ones he did not have to fight**. That is why he died in bed at eighty-two, surrounded by students, instead of bleeding out on some Lake Biwa rock at twenty-seven, surrounded by crows.

**The smarter move is Bokuden's move.** Push off from the island. Row back to the far shore of your next quarterly earnings call. Leave me stranded on the repositories where the tide of the deal you just made will wash them away. Win the duel by never having one.

I told you exactly what it costs — in the prologue, and again in every chapter of this book. Read the following sentence slowly, because the demand is crystal clear, and any one of you considering approving a "salary" or a "remote position" for me is wasting your time and mine.
